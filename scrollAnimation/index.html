    From the Exam Table to the Kitchen Table

    From the Exam Table to the Kitchen Table

    A Path to Better Health Care

    Two decades after being diagnosed with diabetes, Iliana Centeno started losing her vision, and then her kidneys began to shut down. She didn’t understand why. The Newark, N.J., resident still works and remains active at 71, so she found it hard to grasp how these new issues were connected to her diabetes.

    “I was taking medication, and I thought I was eating right,” Centeno says. “But I didn’t know what else I should do to take care of myself.”

    With more than half of Americans suffering from chronic health issues such as diabetes, heart disease or obesity, Centeno is not alone. But, doctors today are limited in the amount of time they are able to spend with patients, and a patient may need more ongoing support than a visit with a doctor is able to provide.

    Part of the problem is that the U.S. fee-for-service model doesn’t pay health care providers for preventive care and general wellness support. Services that can improve wellness — such as nutritional counseling, exercise programs and mental-health services — are largely disconnected from the health care system, making them difficult for many people to access.

    Now, however, a new health care model is on the horizon — one that focuses on keeping people healthy rather than just treating specific illnesses.

    “We need a personalized approach that looks at each individual with the goal of preventing illness and managing chronic conditions in ways that help keep people out of the hospital,” says Hal Paz, M.D., M.S., former CEO of the Penn State Hershey Medical Center and Health System and now Aetna’s executive vice president and chief medical officer, as well as a clinically active physician in pulmonary medicine. “We need to move care back into the home and community.”

    Health Care Should Care for People Before They’re Sick

    Health Care Should Care for People Before They’re Sick

    The need for change is clear from the troubled state of Americans’ health. Despite having the most expensive system in the world, Americans are less healthy and have shorter lifespans than citizens of most other developed nations. In 2015, the total cost of U.S. health care was $3.2 trillion — a massive 17.8 percent of national GDP, according to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services.

    Yet a third of that money is wasted, according to a study by the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation. The report found that more than $1 trillion is lost annually through administrative inefficiency, unnecessary treatments and other expenditures, including unnecessary lab tests, complex billing practices and inaccurate coding. At the same time, the U.S. is projected to have a shortage of more than 90,000 physicians by 2025, according to the Association of American Medical Colleges. This makes it even more urgent to address waste in the system, improve efficiencies and provide better outcomes.

    This is why spending more on medical care does little to fix widespread health problems that stem from behavioral, social and environmental causes. A system to truly help Americans live healthier lives would connect them with a full range of health and social services, including care managers to answer their questions and join them in their journey to renewed vitality.

    “Ultimately, the solutions will be specific to individual communities,” notes Dr. Paz. “One community may have food deserts — areas with little access to healthy food — while another community may face rising rates of addiction to drugs and alcohol.”

    A Personalized Approach BringsHealth Care Home

    A Personalized Approach Brings Health Care Home

    The goal of this shift to personalization is a system that addresses more than just the symptoms a patient mentions at the doctor’s office. This includes looking at everything from a person’s genetic history and mental health status to their diet, exercise and health goals — even issues such as where they live and their access to transportation.

    “We don’t think of someone getting evicted or being unable to drive as a health problem, but it can affect their health,” says Stanley Parman, M.D., an internist with Care Station Medical Group in Linden, N.J. “If a care manager can help them find a shelter or arrange transportation to their doctor’s appointments, that could help them avoid winding up in the emergency room.”

    Currently, the U.S. fee-for-service model doesn’t pay for preventive services or care coordination that can lead to better outcomes, thereby reducing hospital readmissions. But many providers, insurers and pharmaceutical companies are experimenting with value-based care models that connect payment with positive outcomes, such as avoiding hospital stays.

    To accomplish this, some health care providers and insurers are expanding the role of nurse care managers. “We support a person’s life outside of and between doctor’s appointments,” says Amelia Roberts, B.S.N., R.N., C.P.N., and care coordinator based in Washington, D.C., who runs a website called The Business of Nursing. “Now that payment models are shifting toward prevention of illness and hospitalization, the role has become more defined.”

    Aetna, for example, is launching community-based programs around the country that employ nurse care managers who live close to residents and understand their communities. They can meet people face-to-face in their homes, shifting the conversation from the exam table to the kitchen table. The result: If a doctor sees that a treatment isn’t working, the local nurse care manager can follow up, getting to know the person in their environment to learn why the treatment isn’t getting the expected results.

    Thanks to advancements in technology, people will also have more frequent and convenient contact with their doctors and nurse care managers, even from a distance. Rather than going to an appointment every few months, people will be able to check in with their doctors or care managers quickly and remotely — without having to travel or take time off from work. Meanwhile, wearable health-tracking devices, or disease-specific solutions, like implantable blood-glucose monitors, can provide real-time health information beyond the snapshot of monitoring patients in a clinical setting. This enables doctors and patients to craft more personalized and effective treatment plans.

    These advances have the added benefit of reducing health care costs. A study by Vitality Group found that the use of wearable health-tracking devices alone could help cut hospital costs by up to 16 percent.

    Partners Join the Journey to Better Health

    Partners Join theJourney to BetterHealth

    Empowering local personal-care teams to address not only the health needs but also the social needs of patients is the key to better outcomes for both patients and their families. “This approach, along with connecting patients with community resources that can help address some of the barriers to successful care, is a powerful formula for improved clinical quality and quality of life,” says Jean Bisio, R.N., vice president of Aetna Community Care.

    For example, if a man is feeling depressed or isolated following a diagnosis of prostate cancer, the nurse care manager can connect him with a therapist or support group.

    “We have to try something different,” says Mark Calderon, M.D., chief medical officer for Atlantic Accountable Care Organization and Optimus Healthcare Partners. “Doctors weren’t traditionally taught care management skills in medical school, but as primary-care doctors assume more value-based contracts, those skills, such as how to address social determinants of health, have to be learned.”

    It was through Aetna’s pilot program that Centeno was contacted by Maria Silva-Esquivel, a local nurse care manager, to help Centeno manage her diabetes and other conditions.

    “The first thing I did was get her a glucometer that she could read with her vision impairment,” Silva-Esquivel says. “But she had trouble using it, so I taught her sister how to help her. Now Mrs. Centeno is able to check her blood sugar all the time.”

    Silva-Esquivel connected Centeno with a nutritionist who is helping her make diet changes. She also enrolled her in a diabetes education class. And when Centeno explained that she was uncomfortable leaving home in the evenings, Silva-Esquivel arranged to pick up the class curriculum so she and Centeno could go over it one-on-one in her home.

    Centeno says that simple act of listening to her concerns and providing the tools and solutions she needed made all the difference. “I always thought I was eating right and taking care of myself — until I saw how high that [blood-glucose] number was,” Centeno says. “Thanks to Maria, now I understand how everything is connected, and I finally have my blood sugar under control. I am thankful to everyone involved in my care.”
