    A Place Where Global Flavors Meet and Marry

    You can spendlots of time and money traveling the globe in search of distinct international dishes and once-in-lifetime dining experiences — or get all of that in one long weekend in the Canadian province of Ontario.

    Admire Toronto’s skyline as you nosh on Caribbean dishes on a trendy rooftop eatery. Then, head an hour northwest to sip on wines made from rare grapes at a charming vineyard. To top the trip off, head another hour north to Grey County, where you’ll take a helicopter tour of the towering slopes of the Blue Mountains before enjoying a salmon dinner on a beach off the turquoise waters of the Georgian Bay.

    Ontario is food’s best kept secret. Toronto’s food scene has evolved over the years to include homegrown celebrity chefs and creative fusion dishes. And just a couple of hours outside of Ontario’s capital, you’ll find craft wineries and breweries, along with some of the world’s most unique dining experiences.

    Experience Amazing Food Around Ontario

    Taste the World in Toronto

    Toronto is known for its innovative mixing of international cuisine. Tilly Bogle and Martine Joule enjoy a meal at The Rooftop at Broadway Hotel, which has amazing views of Toronto’s skyline and serves international dishes like Jamaican jerk pork ribs and Moroccan minced lamb and beef kefta.

    Any Ontario food journey should begin in Toronto, which is well-known for its variety of international dishes. “The food culture here is amazing,” says John Sinopoli, executive chef at The Rooftop at Broadway Hotel, which serves Jamaican jerk pork ribs and Moroccan minced lamb and beef kefta. “Part of the richness comes from families of different cultures moving here. Then their kids grow up and open restaurants that reflect where they came from.

    Part of the richness comes from families from different cultures moving here.
    John Sinopoli, Executive Chef at The Rooftop at Broadway Hotel

    Get a taste of this in Kensington Market, a trendy neighborhood of restaurants serving international fare. Take your time before settling on a place to eat: The aromas of spices and grilled meats will make you want to walk in every restaurant you pass.

    Seven Lives may catch your eye — and your nose. This cash-only taqueria, which serves Baja-style tacos, is a local favorite. Order the “Gobernador,” which is filled with mounds of smoked marlin, shrimp and cheese. Save room in your belly: Rasta Pasta is a Jamaican-Italian fusion spot a couple doors down. It’s signature dish is gnocchi sautéed in Alfredo or marinara sauce, and served with ackee and salt fish, Jamaica’s national dish.

    Enjoy Unique Food Experiences Just Outside of Toronto

    Grey County in Ontario – home to the Blue Mountain and the blue waters of the Georgian Bay — is stunningly beautiful. There are a number of dining options in Grey County for those who want to have a meal and a great experience. </div>

    If you want a meal and a great experience, Ontario’s Grey County has lots of options. Helicopter outfit Big Blue Air offers a “Fly & Dine” package that includes an aerial tour of the Blue Mountains and the Georgian Bay from Collingwood Airport, which is a couple hours north of Toronto. You’ll stop at Cobble Beach golf resort, where you’ll savor Atlantic salmon and PEI oysters on the patio of the resort’s Sweetwater Restaurant. The restaurant’s panoramic views of the stunningly clear, blue water of the Georgian Bay might trick you into thinking you’re in the Caribbean. On your way back to the airport, you’ll get picture-perfect views of the sun setting behind the mountains.

    Big Blue Air offers a “Fly & Dine” package that includes an aerial tour of the Georgian Bay in a helicopter before stopping at the Cobble Beach waterfront golf resort’s Sweetwater Restaurant for dinner.

    Imagine taking a helicopter ride over the Georgian Bay before landing at a beach for a delicious seafood meal.

    For a unique dinning experience on the ground, head about 20 minutes from the Blue Mountains to Eigensinn Farm. Most of the food served at this outpost of world-renowned German Chef Michael Stadtländer is grown or raised on the property or at nearby farms. “Food from the farm always tastes better,” says Chef Stadtländer, who prepares Asian-fusion dishes that include pan-fried Lake Huron whitefish, pike dumpling and roasted piglet.

    The fresh-farm ingredients are only part of the appeal, though: Stadtländer, and his wife, Nobuyo, treat you as a dear friend. So, too, do your 15 tablemates — all of whom you just met. The dinner isn’t rushed; lingering is encouraged as Nobuyo Stadtländer and her staff serve nine courses over four hours.

    It’s no wonder reservations are made months in advance. If you’re lucky enough to score one, don’t forget the wine: It’s BYOW.

    Sip and See What Ontario Has to Offer

    Shauna White is the head winemaker at Adamo Estate Winery, which is about an hour outside of Toronto.

    About an hour outside Toronto sits Adamo Estate Winery, a modern space with a romantic cellar. While sitting among barrels of aging wines, enjoy sampling the vineyard’s offerings of Chardonnay and Merlot. Be sure to try the 2016 “Frank’s Corner” Vidal, which has a fruity but acidic taste to satisfy dry and sweet wine lovers alike. It’s made from the white hybrid Vidal grape that’s common in Canada — the climate is ideal for it.

    If wine isn’t your fancy, don’t worry: Ontario has a vibrant craft beer scene. Minutes from the Blue Mountains, you’ll find Side Launch Brewing Company. The brewery has a tasting bar with varieties to please any beer lover: Try a wheat beer, a crisp yet full-bodied lager or a more hoppy variety like the Pale Ale Series, with its pungent tropical tang of pineapple, mango and citrus.

    Side Launch gives tours, some of which are led by Michael Hancock, a brew master who has an unparalleled knowledge of beer — and an infectious sense of humor. “If a brewer wants to annoy a winemaker or cider maker, they’ll point out that making beer is more complex,” jokes Hancock, who explains that there’s more work on the front end to make beers, but more work on the backend to make wines and ciders.

    Ontario has a burgeoning number of craft beers, spirits and wines for foodies who appreciate drink pairings with their eats.

    If you’re still thirsty for knowledge about local spirits, visit Toronto’s the Distillery District, a pedestrian-only area with cobblestone streets and buildings that once housed a large whiskey distillery. Now, the district is home to treasures such as the Ontario Spring Water Sake Company.

    The shop carries freshly brewed, unpasteurized Japanese sake, and specialty drinks like the Sakepolitan, made with sake and cranberry juice. Try the ice cream, made from sakekasu, a leftover from sake fermentation. There are three flavors — original, lemon and green tea. Go with the lemon — or better yet, sample all three.

    Check Out Ontario’s Food. Ontario offers one-of-a-kind experiences for the most discerning foodies. There’s so much culinary diversity that you can savor multinational fare — and have unique dining experiences — in one short trip across the Canadian border.
